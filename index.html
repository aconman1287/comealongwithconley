Saigon: Always Be Careful

12.22.15
Coffee is for careful people. But in Ho Chi Minh City, there appears to be a culturally different mindset about being careful. Note: this broad generalization is being cast on an entire city based on my experience with my kind host.

How to be careful:
  • Don’t leave anything outside at night. Even, and maybe especially, a parked and locked motorbike. To Vietnamese, this is just an inherent part of life.
  • Never set any valuable object down in public. Always keep it in your pocket or in your hand. And pocket trumps hand. Anything that is set down is up for grabs. Note: I have not had any problems here. I’ve gone to the restroom at cafes and left my laptop on the table without any fear.
Being careful doesn’t apply to:
  • Being fearless on a motorbike.
    • Immediately after my host reprimanded me for setting my wallet on my bike seat for a half second at the gas station, she sped off into an intersection and across 4 lanes of traffic who were not stopping for her. She expected me to just follow with ease, too. Super careful.
  • Allowing strangers into your house.
    • One morning when Nguyen was preparing a lunch for about 10 people, I was asked to go grab ‘her friend’. As I left, she mentioned she didn’t know what he looked like. I didn’t ask questions (as it was 9am on a Sunday and I was on little sleep) but just headed to get Ezekiel. I found out later that they had never met. Apparently, Nguyen frequents such forums as Couchsurfing and invites people over for meals.

tl;dr :
Personal safety is trumped by the fear of theft in Vietnam.

Losing my bike

12.21.15
Not everyday is a good day while traveling. On a quiet, dreary Sunday in Saigon, I worked out and spent most of the day doing research writing and web design. In the evening, I went and finally picked up a motorbike. Really, I should have done this at the beginning of my stay here for 2 reasons:
  • I love motorbikes. Love love love!
  • The price is the same between a week-long rental and month-long rental.
I head down to the only place I found to contact that had decent prices, VNN Travel, on Bo Vien street, where the backpacker party is. They quickly set me up, and I am off.

I spend the evening catching up with a Remote Year friend before calling it a night and heading back. I return sometime between midnight and 1 am. I lock my bike outside our front door and head for bed.

When I awake (early I might add), I head downstairs. As I’m pouring a glass of water, I look out front and don’t see my bike. I assume someone moved it this way or that to get around it (though I’m not sure that would be easy with it locked). As I scour my quaint Vietnamese neighborhood, I see no signs of it. Then, a neighborhood mother that runs the shop in the square approaches me telling me something. I have no idea what she is trying to communicate, but she is pointing north into the distance. While I don’t comprehend anything from this interaction, I do infer that she knows something about my bike, and I assume that’s a good sign.

In my frantic 7am haze, I decide to walk around the block briefly to just check around and figure out what I should do. When I return, I decide to call Nguyen (my host) to consult her. When she hears what happens, she freaks out and heads my way. When she arrives, she hears my story, assesses the situation, talks to neighbors, and we conclude that the bike is stolen.

What that means for me is $2000 to the rental company to replace it. Well, they say the bike is worth $2000, but it is not. I could buy a comparable bike for $300 - $400.

Nguyen wants to call the police. I tell her to hold on a minute first and ask her to speak with the mother who had tried to tell me something earlier. They talk, and Nguyen comes back inside. What she learned was that the Vietnamese police officer who does a sweep of the neighborhood every night took my bike. Apparently after I went to bed, he showed up at our place and rang the doorbell to alert me to bring the bike inside. I did not hear it. The lady said that 5 policemen show up and are ringing the doorbell for about two hours! When I don’t come down, they take the bike to the police station. The lady who knew all of this was up around this time and told the cops that two foreigners were staying at the place. Great…


Nguyen and I start to formulate a plan. She is worried about a few things:
  1. The police now know that foreigners are staying here, and we are supposed to be registered so they know. This could mean a fine or worse.
  2. Nguyen’s house is not registered to be able to host as a business, so if they don’t believe that we are just friends, she could face fines.
  3. The house does not have a ’temporary registration’ since Nguyen technically lives down the street with her mother and daughter. If she wants to avoid the penalties for #1 and #2, she would need to temporary register this place as her home and pay back taxes on it. And possibly a fine.
  4. I will have to pay a fine for being out past curfew. In these backstreet Vietnamese neighborhoods, there is a curfew to prevent people from disturbing neighbors at all hours. It does not seem to be enforced much, but if you’re caught doing something else, they can fine you for this.
  5. I will have to pay a fine for parking my bike outside. This is simply not what you’re supposed to do. Everything goes inside at night.
I’m worried that this may cost a lot of money and result in Nguyen being in bad standing with the police. She decides to head to the police station to see what she can do, but she returns shortly because the police are in their weekly Monday morning meeting. She calls the police guard who protects the neighborhood, and he agrees to meet at a coffee shop.

Nguyen, the saint that she is, cancels a couple of meetings for the day and heads to negotiate with the police officer. The long and short of it is that the police officer asks for 500,000 dong (~$20). He tells her to meet him at the police station at 2pm with the vehicle registration card, and he’ll see what he can do.

She returns, explains the situation, and now we need to obtain the vehicle registration card. We call the shop I rented from. We have a problem. The rental shop owner bought the bike under the table to avoid taxes on the transaction. That means the vehicle registration card was not in his name but the previous owner’s name. So now if we need the person whose name is on the registration card to get the bike out, we may need to involve yet another person and potentially open them up to fines and back taxes because of their transaction. Oh my freaking gosh. I’ve opened up pandora’s box of fines and taxes for simply parking outside and not knowing there was a rule against it.

Ok. Ok. We get the vehicle registration and wait for 2pm. As the time approaches, Nguyen comes up with a brilliant idea to take two bottles of sake of mine (that were free from a restaurant a week before) to bring to the police chief as a gift to smooth things over. She tells me to wait again because if they know I’m a foreigner from the US, they may ask for more money (since they assume I have plenty).

She leaves.

Within 15 minutes, she calls.

“Have Tom bring you here to pick up the bike.”

YES. We head down to the station, grab the bike, and take off as a family of three on our motorbikes. All our worries had been avoided.

tl;dr:
With a $20 and two bottle of sake bribe, I was able to recover my bike from the Vietnamese police.

The land of opportunity

12.17.15
Today, again I learned some amazing things in Ho Chi Minh. I had lunch with my host, Nguyen, and my roommate Tom, and amazing conversations ensued.
Background:
Tom - from Asturias, Spain but was living in London before heading to Kuala Lumpur 6 months ago and working as a paid volunteer for a camp outside of the city
Nguyen - lovely host from Ho Chi Minh who runs her own fast food / supermarket style place (well helps her cousins, who she sold it to, run it) and hosts so many people in her home
We chatted about the prospect of living in the US. What Nguyen thought:
  • Education is better. Education through high school is completely free.
  • To get into National Vietnamese University, have to be 1 out of 1000.
  • If you don’t get into National, you can go to private, but it’s more expensive (compare about 8million dong to 20million dong)
  • When you graduate from private, its very hard to get a job.
  • Nguyen worked hard when studying but didn’t get a government job. Her friend, who did not study hard, her father was in the government, and now she has a cozy government job and is rich.
  • In the US, its a lot easier to afford to have a car.
  • You can get healthcare much easier. Even if you don’t have insurance, the doctors will treat you.
  • If your flight is delayed, Americans will get a hotel to stay the night. Not so if you’re from a different country.

To my phone, All my Love (pt. 2) [or 12 Gather in Mobile Store because of Foreigner]

12.06.15
In my last post, I confessed my love (read: addiction) to my mobile phone. That was simply a super long preamble to a hilarious story about me trying to recharge my SIM card in Vietnam. Said story ensues:
Did I accomplish my mission? Yes.

Did it take an entire afternoon spent at a Viettel (mobile carrier) store? Yes.

Were 12 bystanders and staff involved in the incident? Yes.

Was Bill Murray present during all of this? No.

I had to get some more data for my new iphone6, now named Tobias (cuz he's a never-nude). I'd already ripped through my initial 3GB of data in Vietnam and without wifi at my abode, I needed at least 12GB more to use my phone as a hotspot for late night nothings online.
The journey begins

I have the ability to simply recharge my current SIM card, so that's my go-to move. Ought to be easy enough, right? Well, I ask my host, Nguyen (who I adore), where the nearest mobile shop is. She points about three houses down from us but quickly points out that he doesn't speak English. Hmm...I think. I want to make sure I'm getting the right thing, and between Nguyen's partial English and this guy's zero English, I'm already worried. But...she insists...so I head down there ready to make sure this works before I walk away. Then what happens? I walk away a bit disappointed. While it seems to work...I believe the carrier was still throttling my usage. Grrrr!!!
Plan B.

Time for a taxi to my current carrier's shop. Taxi secured. 20 minutes later through Ho Chi Minh traffic, I arrive. Boom. There it is. In all its glory. And its still being constructed. I walk down the street to a Mobiphone shop (another provider). I sit down with the man inside who looks utterly irritated that he has to deal with someone who speaks 0 Vietnamese. I don't blame him at all - I would be equally as impatient. Needless to say, we don't get very far. Apparently, this guy doesn't work on commission. I leave.
Plan C.

"Screw it!" I say to myself. I decide to head back toward the large Viettel (yet another provider's) store closer to my home. Back to the taxi that takes me back toward my home!

Ok. I've landed. I walk into this large turquoise and yellow store littered with motorbikes blocking the entrance, thinking to myself, "Surely someone speaks perfect English in this modern-looking establishment." Yeah yeah yeah - you entitled wanker! I don't feel like anyone in Vietnam needs to know English...but since many do, I'm conditioned to having such an expectation. Back to the matter at hand, please...

I'm immediately greeted by two very friendly, smiling female staff who speak a little English. I indicated that I'm looking for a new SIM card, so they walk me over to another employee and have me sit down. They show me a few options, and through the use of Google Translate, we begin to make some progress on understanding what I might be able to purchase.
Now for the stir.

From here on out, the story becomes hazy, so I'll describe it in stream of consciousness...how it happened to me.


turn left to someone leaning in asking me if we can connect on. head goes to the right back to the staff across the counter. facebook and skype so she can practice her english. tapped on the shoulder behind me. 120,000 dong for 12GB, sir. can I make. sorry I can't help you more. phone calls? smiles. two more staff show up behind me. I stand. I turn left and right. are there really 12 people standing around me in this store as I am seemingly conducting some sort of 1-man show? one lucky bystander: what's the problem sir. oh, you speak English. Thank you! I messed up my order twice? 12 GB and calls? no. a lot of indications that I may be able to get what I want. that will be 400,000 dong. dong. dong. dong. my friend over here thinks you are cute. cam on. come on! Oh, it's for my phone. What is happening, miss? smiles.

Can We JuSt StArT OvEr? howmanytimesareyougoingtoscanmypassport?! #gigsandgiggles I think I spun around one too many times. what time is it will i get any work done today did I miss lunch with Nguyen are these people laughing with me or at me do I smell will I ever get a SIM card?????? Don't worry Tobias. Don't worry.
The show ends.

I got my SIM card. I made two friends who I can keep in contact with. Tobias is fulfilled. And hopefully, everyone got a kick out of that 2 hour experience.

To my Phone, All my Love pt. 1

11.28.15
A huge modern-day necessity when traveling and working: having access to data on your phone. It's amazing how much my phone usage has gone up since I left theDonaldland. You quickly realize that you rely on it for so many things you didn't think of before.

Let me walk you through this quickly.

As a late 20s-something, each afternoon / evening I say to myself, "Hey Andrew, what are you going to eat tonight?" "Well, that depends Andrew," I say to myself. "Are you attending any events tonight or are there any that are serving free food?" There's not, and I'm glad, as I could use a night to chillax (chilling and relaxing at the same time - similar to a double rainbow but more meaningful - more on that in a separate post). So instead I ask myself, "Do we have anything in the fridge?" Yes, but I'm not sure what. I decide instead to head to Cooper's Tavern on the square to meet a friend for dinner after work and before heading back to my place.

This story seems simple enough, but now let's add in one new bit: I'm traveling in a foreign country by myself.

As a late 20s-something, each afternoon / evening I say to myself, "Hey Andrew, what are you going to eat tonight?" "Well, that depends Andrew," I say to myself. "Can I cook food in my place and do I have food there to cook?" No - I can't, and there's not.If I could and there were, I would have previously had to have gone grocery shopping at a market that I look up on my phone, decide is legitimate, and then navigate toward it. Since that isn't what happened and because I want to see the city more, I decide that I'll go out for dinner this evening. I ask myself, "Where should I go out to eat?" This requires me taking out my phone, searching for places for good reviews on Foursquare, finding it in Google Maps for directions (being sure to use Google Maps; not Apple), then soliciting a ride there via either GrabTaxi or Uber.

Notice the difference? Of course you do. My phone is like a great friend:

O sweet mobile of mine!
Oh, how you answer all my questions.
My fears recoil at a glance of your strength.
You are my mighty sword which I wield from in my front pocket.
You know my name and treat me with utter respect!
I use you constantly and let you die repeatedly without thought of what I could be doing for you.
What do you want?
What are your dreams?
When will your voice ring in my ear, loyal friend?
'Tis thee that hath empowered me so.
'Tis thee that hath liberated my soul.
As your battery life dwindles this evening before I plug you in to awaken,
Goodnight sweet prince.

What I've been up to so far

08.24.15

I got this whole blog thing started pretty late - but I’m here, and it’s happening. I’ve officially been on the road now for 49 days (since July 4). I’ve been through quite a few cities since then:

Madison - Chicago - Dublin - Milan - Venice - Villach - Ljubljana - Stahovica - Zagreb - Plitvice - Matavun - Luce - Lake Bled - Rijeka - Cavtat - Dubrovnik - Kotor - Tivat - Hvar.


Though it feels like I’ve barely been in Cavtat, we will be heading to Istanbul next Sunday - away from the Dalmatian Coast paradise and onward to where East meets West in one of the 5 biggest cities in the world.

It seems like I’ve barely been here, but it also feels like I’ve been here forever. The hardest thing to learn on this trip seems to be how to slow down, say no to things, and just live and breathe. Things are moving so fast, and with so much going on, you become a bit like a kid in a candy store running around trying to grab at everything before its time to leave.

I’m excited to relax a bit more and sink into a better routine. It seems that the entire group has that same sentiment. The honeymoon period is ending, and people seem ready to start pursuing their goals for this trip. Almost everyone intends to grow professionally while traveling, and with all the other excitement, its been hard to get started.
